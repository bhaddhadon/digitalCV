Order Stacking Allowance System based on SLA

Problem Statement

The legacy order stacking system operated on a simple quota-based rule: a driver (e.g. a motorcyclist in Kuala Lumpur or a van driver in Hong Kong) could carry up to 2 priority or regular orders and a maximum of 4 orders in total across all tiers (priority, regular, and pooling — the cheapest tier). This hard cap made no consideration of time, distance, route, or order destination proximity. As a result, the system was inflexible: it could not differentiate between a driver taking two nearby orders and one taking two orders in opposite directions, treating both scenarios identically regardless of the actual impact on delivery time.

Solution

A shift from quota-based stacking to a Service-Level-Agreement (SLA) driven model. Instead of capping the number of orders a driver can carry, the system determines stacking eligibility by simulating the projected delivery timeline for each order in the current stack if a new order were to be added. A driver is permitted to accept an additional order as long as no existing order in the stack would be delayed beyond its respective SLA threshold.

  • 1. Route and ETA simulation for each candidate order addition, incorporating real-time traffic and spatial data.
  • 2. Per-tier SLA enforcement: priority orders are held to stricter latency budgets, while regular and pooling orders carry more lenient thresholds.
  • 3. Dynamic stacking decisions made at the time of order assignment, replacing static quota rules.

Key Business Objectives:

  • Stackable rate per order tier (priority / regular / pooling)
  • Average stack size increase post-rollout
  • Shorter delivery time for stacked orders compared to legacy system
Benefits
  • Greater operational flexibility for regular and pooling orders, enabling higher stacking rates and improved fleet efficiency
  • Stricter, more reliable delivery guarantees for priority orders
  • Reduced reliance on arbitrary hard caps — stacking decisions are grounded in real delivery impact
  • Enables the business to tune SLA thresholds independently per order tier as market conditions evolve
Evaluation
  • Priority order SLA compliance maintained or improved despite allowing uncapped stacking
  • Measurable increase in average orders per trip for regular and pooling tiers
  • No significant degradation in customer satisfaction scores for non-priority tiers
Next Steps
  • Fine-tune SLA thresholds per market and vehicle type based on observed delivery patterns
  • Explore oder bundeling, having observed driver-initiated stacking route
